Foundation

Four Premises Before Any Number

These are the structural facts that should constrain every pricing decision. Get these wrong and the number doesn't matter.

1. SimGym is infrastructure, not software

The mental model for buyers is Datadog, LaunchDarkly, or Sentry — not a SaaS tool like Notion or Linear. Infrastructure tools get priced on what they protect (compute, features, errors, agents), not on who uses them. Per-seat pricing is wrong for this category because there are only 2–5 AI engineers per company, which caps your ACV at a level that doesn't reflect the value delivered. Sentry doesn't charge per developer who reads error logs. Datadog doesn't charge per engineer who looks at dashboards.

2. The buyer is different from the user

The user is the AI engineer running simulations daily. The buyer is the CTO or VP Eng who cares about prevented incidents and engineering velocity. Your pricing must justify itself to the buyer in their language (cost of incidents, engineering leverage, reliability SLAs), not the user's language (test coverage, simulation runs). This split matters enormously for packaging: free tier is for users; paid tiers must speak to buyers.

3. You are in the "insurance" category of developer tooling

Nobody wants to pay for insurance. They pay because the cost of not having it is catastrophic and visible. Your pricing experiments must surface this — pricing SimGym without making the economic case for the cost of not having it will consistently anchor customers to low numbers.

4. Early pricing sets category expectations you'll live with for years

Underpricing early-stage deals is very hard to undo. Enterprise buyers remember what they paid. Design partners become your references and case studies — the price they paid sets market expectations. Price for the value the product will deliver at maturity, not for the MVP you have today.

Dimension 1

The Right Value Metric

The value metric is the unit your price scales on. It must be: (a) correlated with the value customers receive, (b) easy to understand, (c) growing as customers get more value, and (d) not punishing customers for getting more use out of the product.

Research across 500+ B2B SaaS companies shows that companies that correctly identify their core value metric see 10–20% faster revenue growth compared to arbitrary or convenience-based metrics. Hybrid models (base fee + usage) are now used by 61% of SaaS companies and deliver the highest median NRR (~105%).

Recommendation

Primary metric: number of production agents under active simulation coverage. Secondary mechanism: simulation run volume as an overage or tier gate (not the primary driver). This mirrors how Datadog (hosts), LaunchDarkly (flags/MAUs), and Sentry (error events) structure their value metrics in developer infrastructure.

Practically, "agents under coverage" means: agent pipelines actively instrumented and running through SimGym's simulation environment. A company with 3 customer-facing AI agents counts as 3. This number grows naturally as they build their product — which is exactly when they're also raising their next round and have budget.

The Comparison Frame That Works With Buyers

When a buyer hesitates on price, the right frame is: "What did your last production incident cost you in engineering time and customer trust?" Not: "Here's what you get for $X." Infrastructure tools sell on avoided cost, not feature lists.

78.3% of sales reps missed quota in 2025 — not because reps were underperforming, but because quota was set wrong. Poor quota design alone costs a mid-market company $500k–$2M/yr in revenue predictability, over-compensation, and rep turnover. The economic stakes of GTM simulation are just as large as agent simulation.

What PLG Means for SimGym Specifically

SimGym's PLG motion has one structural challenge: the "aha moment" requires catching a real regression, which takes time. This is not a 2-minute demo product. That makes a time-limited free trial more effective than a freemium free tier — because free trials have a defined evaluation window and a natural conversion point, while freemium can linger indefinitely without urgency.

PLG Design Recommendation

Offer a 14-day free trial with full access (not a feature-limited free tier). The goal is to get them connected, run their first simulation, and — ideally — catch something. If SimGym catches a real issue in the trial, that is the conversion event. Add a "catch report" email that fires when something is flagged: that email, with the description of what would have happened, is your best conversion driver.

The PLG → Sales Hand-off

43% of SaaS companies now use hybrid models. The pattern that works: PLG self-serve for individual engineers at Seed/Series A companies (ACV under $25k); sales-assisted or sales-led for Series B+ and mid-market (ACV $25k+). The product creates demand; the sales team captures expansion.

Rationale for These Numbers

  • Team at $21.6k/yr is below most procurement thresholds (~$25k), enabling a CTO to sign without a 6-week vendor review. It's anchored at ~10% of the economic value of 2–3 prevented incidents at a Series A company ($80–200k/yr). Comparable dev infra tools (LaunchDarkly, Sentry) land here for this segment.
  • Scale at $50.4k/yr sits in the established mid-market range for developer infrastructure tools. It's ~5% value capture on a Series B company preventing $1M+/yr in incidents. It creates a strong upgrade path from Team (2.3x price jump for 3x agents).
  • Enterprise at $80–200k/yr is below Gong ($85k minimum) and Clari ($100k+), but comparable to Fullcast and Datadog in this segment. The range is wide because mid-market deals have high variance — don't anchor the floor too low or you'll lose credibility in negotiations.

Dimension 7

Design Partner Pricing

Design partners are a distinct deal structure — they're not just early customers, they're co-builders. The pricing logic is different: they're paying for access, influence, and a locked rate, not just product access.

Core Principles

  • Always charge. Free pilots convert at under 10% (Heavybit). Paid pilots convert at 40–70%+ when properly structured. Sierra (First Round-backed) charged 10–20% of total ACV upfront and achieved 100% pilot-to-paid conversion. Strella ran 12 paid pilots and converted all 12 — yielding $1.6M ARR in year 1.
  • The pilot fee must be below the "needs approval" threshold. At Series A/B companies, the CTO or VP Eng can spend $10k without a procurement review. Keep the pilot fee under this threshold to remove a decision gate.
  • The annual price must have a visible and credible future list price above it. The discount only has value if the future price is believable. Commit to your roadmap pricing in writing.

Why $6–8k pilot fee (not $4–5k)

Research shows pilot fees at 15–25% of target ACV optimize conversion. At a $22k annual target, 25% = $5.5k; 33% = $7.3k. The $6–8k range is in the sweet spot — high enough to signal commitment (and qualify out tire-kickers), low enough that a CTO can approve it without a formal vendor review cycle. The pilot fee going 100% toward Year 1 makes the net cost of the annual feel like $14–16k, not $22k — which lowers the psychological barrier at contract time.

The Day 45 Conversion Conversation

Surface this at Day 45, not Day 60. By then you should have at least one documented catch to anchor the renewal on. The script:

"We're two weeks from the end of the pilot. We caught [specific regression] — which would have affected [X users for Y hours, or cost $Z to debug]. Based on that, what does the path look like to move to the annual contract? Anyone else we need to loop in?"

Never allow open-ended extensions. Extensions without a new fee signal a soft no — 60–90% of indefinitely extended pilots never convert (McKinsey data). Force the decision. If they need more time, charge for it.

Dimension 8

What to Test and In What Order

You don't have enough data yet to know if these numbers are right. Here's how to sequence your experiments.

Phase 0: Design Partners (First 90 days)

Sales-led Test with 3–5 companies.

  • Hypothesis to test: Will a Series A/B AI engineering team pay $6–8k upfront for a 60-day pilot, and convert at $22k/yr annual?
  • Success metric: 2+ paid pilots signed; 1+ converted to annual before Day 60.
  • What to vary: Try $5k pilot fee with one company, $8k with another. Watch for objection patterns — are they objecting to the fee, the timeline, or the value metric?
  • Falsification criteria: If 3+ prospects walk at the pilot fee, you have a price signal. If they walk at the annual, you have a value signal.

Phase 1: Tier Validation (Months 3–9)

Hybrid After 2+ paid annual contracts signed.

  • Hypothesis to test: Does the Team tier ($21.6k/yr, up to 10 agents) convert self-serve from free trial?
  • Success metric: 15–30% free trial → paid conversion within 30 days of trial expiry (this is the benchmark for time-limited free trials).
  • What to watch: Where do free users hit limits? If they're hitting the run limit before the agent limit, the tiers are calibrated wrong. Adjust accordingly.
  • PQL definition: Any company that connects 2+ agents AND runs 200+ simulations in the trial window — that company's CTO gets a personal outreach from you, not an automated email.

Phase 2: Mid-Market Pricing (Months 9–18)

Sales-led Once you have 5–10 annual customers.

  • Hypothesis to test: Can you land a mid-market deal at $75–100k/yr with a 60-day enterprise evaluation?
  • Success metric: 1 Enterprise contract signed before month 18.
  • What to vary: Try entering at $50k floor with one prospect, $80k with another. Watch: do they negotiate on price, or on features/terms? Price objections = wrong number. Feature objections = right number, wrong product.

The Most Important Early Pricing Signal

Watch the ratio of "what they budgeted" to "what you asked." If every customer comes in with a $12k budget and you're asking $22k, you need to either adjust price or adjust who you're targeting. If they come in with a $50k budget and you're asking $22k, you're leaving money on the table and giving them no incentive to take you seriously as an enterprise vendor. The delta between their budget and your ask is your most honest pricing data.

One Rule for Design Partners

Cap design partners at 5–8 per tier. After that, retire the design partner rate and move to list price. Design partner pricing exists to validate the model and generate references — it is not a permanent segment. Commit to a sunset date for the design partner rate in every contract you sign.
